EV Charging Site Project Plan

Executive Summary

This document is the master, traceable closeout deliverable for an EV charging site electrical project. It presents a complete, phase-structured record of how an EV charging installation was engineered, reviewed, submitted, revised, and finalized, with every key decision tied back to its inputs and supporting evidence.

The plan is organized into eight phases (Phase 1 through Phase 8). Within each phase, the document identifies the prerequisite inputs and how they were provided, establishes a stable evidence index (so files can be retrieved unambiguously), and summarizes how each deliverable was created (calculations, decision records, compilation/QA logs). It also preserves an audit trail of external review cycles—authority plan check and serving utility review—by capturing submission packages, receipts, comment logs, response letters, and approvals/finaled evidence.

The intent is not to restate every technical detail in prose, but to provide a defensible chain of custody: what information was relied on, how assumptions were verified and frozen, what artifacts were produced, and what evidence demonstrates acceptance at each checkpoint.

For readers who are not familiar with how electrical work is reviewed and validated in California, the Introduction section provides the operational context—how AHJs and utilities function as separate audit systems, why “written code” and “enforced code” diverge in practice, and why traceability and early risk characterization materially affect delivery outcomes.

Introduction

In California, electrical engineering work is validated externally by two independent authorities: the Authority Having Jurisdiction (AHJ) and the serving electrical utility. AHJs enforce the adopted California Electrical Code and local amendments through plan check and inspection. Utilities are not code enforcers; they manage grid and asset risk, reviewing added load, service impacts, fault duty, and—in EV projects—the credibility of any claimed load-management scheme. These entities do not coordinate with each other and do not participate in design. They review finished artifacts only.

In the San Francisco Bay Area, the AHJ is typically the city or county building department responsible for electrical permits. A multifamily EV project in Palo Alto, for example, is reviewed by the City of Palo Alto – Building Division, while a similar project nearby may face a different department with different amendments, reviewer expectations, and enforcement thresholds. The serving utility runs a parallel review with different criteria and timelines. Same written code, materially different enforcement outcomes.

Both AHJs and utilities operate as audit systems. They do not accept undocumented reasoning, verbal explanations, or implied engineering intent. Anything not written, indexed, and traceable is treated as nonexistent. Most comments and deficiencies are not about incorrect calculations but about failure to demonstrate how inputs flowed into outputs and whether assumptions were verified, frozen, and consistently applied. This is why written code ≠ enforced code in practice, and why designing purely to the most conservative interpretation everywhere results in systematic over-engineering.

Over-engineering is not a neutral safety margin in California; it is economically destructive. The state faces a chronic scarcity of licensed EEs and especially PEs, which drives very high labor costs. Every unnecessary design iteration, oversizing decision, or avoidable service upgrade consumes scarce senior hours that cannot be amortized. When firms respond to regulatory uncertainty with blanket conservatism, they burn the most expensive resource they have and compress margins without meaningfully reducing approval risk.

Modeling each AHJ—what they actually enforce, what they routinely question, and where they allow judgment—is therefore critical. Precise alignment to enforcement reality allows designs that are code-compliant, defensible, and no more complex than necessary. This reduces PE touch time, limits redesign cycles, and keeps projects within a labor envelope that is survivable given market scarcity.

Utilities must be modeled with the same rigor. Written utility standards, handbooks, or greenbooks do not reflect how load increases, EMS claims, or service constraints are actually reviewed in practice. Utilities differ materially in what they flag, what they defer, what they require upfront, and what they reopen later in the process. Treating utilities as deterministic rule engines leads to the same failure mode as over-interpreting code: unnecessary conservatism, late surprises, and unplanned rework. Capturing utility behavior—what documentation they demand, how they evaluate managed load, and where they apply discretion—is essential to avoiding service upgrades and uncontrolled scope growth.

Profitability depends on this discipline starting early. Correctly characterizing a project’s regulatory and utility risk at intake—based on service conditions, load posture, AHJ enforcement behavior, and utility sensitivity—and fitting it into a canonical, repeatable form is what enables accurate pricing. When risk is explicitly classified rather than discovered midstream, fees can reflect true exposure, delivery plans can be matched to risk class, and engineers can execute with confidence that the work is achievable within tolerable margins. In California EE practice, traceability, AHJ and utility modeling, and early risk canonicalization are prerequisites for operating profitably under scarcity and scrutiny.

14 Addendum C: Energy Management System (EMS) Architecture & Control Logic

14.1 Core Hardware Components

While the One-Line Diagram (P3.1) focuses on power flow, the EMS “nervous system” consists of the following components that facilitate the Sense-Think-Act loop:

Current Transformer (CT) Array: High-precision “clamshell” sensors installed on the main service busbars. These measure the magnetic field of the total building draw in real-time.

EMS Controller / Local Gateway: An industrial-grade microcomputer (typically DIN-rail mounted in the EV subpanel). It serves as the local intelligence hub, capable of operating even during internet outages.

Grid Communication Interface: A dedicated bridge (often Modbus or RS485) that translates the analog signals from the CTs into digital data for the controller.

14.2 Hardware & Software Interfaces

The system operates across three primary interface layers:

Physical Interface (CT-to-Gateway): Shielded twisted-pair wiring that transmits the live amperage readings from the main switchgear to the EMS controller.

Local Network Interface (Gateway-to-Charger): Usually an Ethernet or robust Mesh Wi-Fi network using the OCPP (Open Charge Point Protocol) to send throttling commands to each of the 8 stations.

Cloud API Interface: An encrypted outbound connection (Cellular or WAN) used for remote monitoring, firmware updates, and reporting.

14.3 The Role of the Cloud

The Cloud does not perform the real-time load shedding—that is handled locally for safety and speed. Instead, the Cloud provides:

Priority Profiles: Allows the owner to designate specific chargers for “VIP” or “Fleet” priority via a web dashboard.

Data Logging: Records proof for the utility (Taylor Nguyen) that the 250A cap was never exceeded.

Latency Compensation: Monitors network health to ensure commands are reaching chargers within the required 2-second window.

14.4 Control Logic: Maintaining the 250A Max

The EMS maintains the limit through a continuous mathematical loop:

  1. Measurement: Total Site Load (Ptotal) is measured by CTs.

  2. Calculation: Available EV Power (Pavail) = 250A − Pbuilding.

  3. Command: If Ptotal > 245A, the EMS sends a “SetChargingProfile” command via OCPP to all 8 chargers.

  4. Throttling: Chargers reduce their pilot signal (PWM), forcing the cars to drop current instantly.

  5. Fail-Safe: If the Gateway loses contact with the CTs or the Cloud, all chargers default to 6A (minimum) to ensure the main breaker never trips.

15 Addendum D: Three-Phase Distribution & Phase Balancing Logic

15.1 The “Wye” Distribution Strategy

The project utilizes a 208Y/120V Three-Phase Wye configuration. This is the standard “Workhorse” of commercial buildings in California.

The 3P Feeder: The wires coming from the main service to the EV Subpanel (Panel B) carry all three phases (A, B, and C). This allows us to pull power from three different “lanes” of the grid simultaneously.

The 1P Load: Because the vehicles are North American spec (Single-Phase), they can only bridge two of these phases at a time to get 208V (e.g., A-B, B-C, or C-A).

15.2 The “Hidden” Balancing Logic

To prevent a “Phase Imbalance”—which causes heat in the neutral wire and stresses the utility transformer—the 8 chargers are distributed across the phases in a 3-3-2 staggered pattern:

  • Group 1 (Stations 1, 4, 7): Connected to Phase A and B.

  • Group 2 (Stations 2, 5, 8): Connected to Phase B and C.

  • Group 3 (Stations 3, 6): Connected to Phase C and A.

By spreading the chargers this way, we ensure that if all 8 cars are charging at full tilt, the load on any single phase (A, B, or C) stays relatively equal, maximizing the 250A headroom provided by the utility.

15.3 Why the Schedule Looks “Messy”

A standard Panel Schedule is organized by Circuit Numbers, not by Phase Group. This can make the balancing logic difficult to see at a glance:

Standard Numbering: Circuits 1 & 3 are Phase A-B; Circuits 5 & 7 are Phase B-C; Circuits 9 & 11 are Phase C-A.

The Visual Jumble: Because breakers are installed vertically (1, 3, 5, 7…), the “Phase A” column on the schedule will have values for Station 1 and Station 3, but nothing for Station 2.

Verification: To confirm the balance, ignore the individual rows and look at the KVA Totals at the bottom of the schedule. In a balanced design, the totals for Phase A, B, and C should be within 5% of each other.

15.4 Physical Integration Note

The 250A EVSP Feeder will utilize the contiguous spare breaker section (Slots 24-42) of the MDP. This avoids the need for a “service tap” or external “gutter” connection, which are common points of failure in retrofits of this age. The large-frame 250A breaker will be centered on the bus to ensure even heat dissipation across the 800A main chassis.

16 Addendum E: Utility Integration & Power Contractual Realities

16.1 Section A: The Requirement for Utility Notification (Phase 6)

A common misconception is that if a building’s 800A Main Distribution Panel (MDP) has 250A of “spare” physical room, no utility coordination is required. However, the Utility (PG&E/SCE) must be involved for three critical reasons that exist outside the building’s walls.

16.1.1 Asset Thermal Stress (The Continuous Load Factor)

Standard building loads (elevators, HVAC, lighting) are intermittent. The utility sizes their neighborhood transformers based on a “diversity factor”—assuming everyone won’t use max power at the same time.

The Conflict: EV charging is a Continuous Load (defined by the NEC as 3+ hours).

The Risk: A transformer can handle a 550A “spike” from your elevators for 15 minutes easily. It may not be able to handle a sustained 800A draw for 8 hours without the internal cooling oil overheating, leading to a “burn-out” that can plunge the entire block into darkness.

16.1.2 Grid Visibility & Rule 21 Compliance

Under California Rule 21, utilities are tasked with maintaining grid stability.

Blind Spot: If 10 buildings on the same street all add 250A of “hidden” EV load, the utility’s local distribution circuit will become unstable.

The Requirement: By submitting a Load Letter (Phase 6), you ensure Taylor Nguyen (the utility rep) can update their grid model. If the transformer is too small, the utility is often required to upgrade it at their own cost under Rule 29 (EV Infrastructure Rules).

16.1.3 Secondary Service Wires

The wires running from the street to your building (the Service Drop) were sized based on your building’s original projected demand, not necessarily its maximum 800A breaker rating. Increasing baseline consumption by 250A creates persistent heat that can melt underground insulation if the utility hasn’t confirmed the wire gauge is sufficient for continuous duty.

16.2 Section B: Contracted Service (kVA) vs. Physical Rating (Amps)

While you “contract” a service in kVA (kilovolt-amperes), your physical equipment is rated in Amps. This leads to the “Billing vs. Safety” gap.

16.2.1 The Service Agreement ID (SAID)

When your building was built, the owner signed a Service Agreement for a specific kVA capacity.

Contracted Demand: You may be paying a rate for 400 kVA of capacity.

Physical Peak: Your 800A MDP allows for ~288 kVA.

The Gap: If you increase your usage to 250 kVA, you are still under your 800A breaker, but you have exceeded your Contracted Demand. This triggers a “Change of Service” request to avoid excessive demand penalties and to legally update your power contract.

16.2.2 Understanding the kVA Billing Model

Utility billing for commercial/multifamily sites relies on two distinct charges:

Energy Charge (kWh): How much total fuel you used.

Demand Charge (kVA/kW): The “width” of the pipe you used at your single busiest 15-minute window.

Impact: Adding 8 chargers significantly widens your “pipe” usage. By involving the utility, you can often move to an EV-specific rate (like PG&E’s BEV rate), which reduces the kVA demand charges in exchange for slightly higher kWh costs during peak times.

16.2.3 Real-Time kVA Management via EMS

The EMS (Energy Management System) hardware acts as the bridge between your Physical Amps and your Contracted kVA.

The Logic: The EMS is programmed with your “Contracted Max kVA.”

The Action: Even if your 250A breaker has “room,” if the total building kVA starts to approach the contract limit, the EMS will throttle the chargers to stay within the billing “sweet spot,” protecting the owner from massive overage fees.

16.3 Summary: Why the Load Letter is your “Legal Shield”

By providing a Load Letter that mentions your EMS, you are telling the utility: “We are increasing our kVA demand, but our software will prevent us from ever exceeding the 800A safety limit of our MDP and your transformer.” This usually grants you an immediate “Approval to Proceed” without expensive infrastructure upgrades.

17 Addendum F: Fault Current & Arc Flash

This addendum explains why this project does not require a full engineered fault current study or an arc flash study at the design stage, and how AIC (Ampere Interrupting Capacity) ratings satisfy code and inspection requirements.

17.1 The “Dwelling Unit” Exception (NEC 110.24)

Under the National Electrical Code (NEC 110.24), which California adopts, there is a specific exception for residential dwellings.

The Rule: Service equipment in buildings other than dwelling units must be field-marked with the maximum available fault current.

The Exception: Because this project is a multifamily dwelling with a service likely under 1000A (the site has an 800A MDP), the strict requirement for engineered fault current labeling is often waived or simplified. The AHJ may accept the approach described in §17.3 (AIC ratings) in lieu of a separate fault study document.

17.2 Arc Flash is an “Operational” Study, Not a “Design” Study

Arc flash calculations (NFPA 70E) are designed to protect workers who maintain equipment while it is energized.

The Timing: In most California jurisdictions, an arc flash study is considered a post-construction maintenance requirement, not a permit-submission requirement.

The Data Gap: To perform an accurate arc flash study, the EE needs the exact manufacturer, model, and trip-settings of every breaker installed. Since equipment has not been purchased yet (Phase 7), any calculation done in Phase 3 would be a “best guess” and legally invalid for OSHA-style labeling. A proper arc flash study is therefore deferred until as-built conditions and installed equipment are known.

17.3 AIC Ratings Handle the Safety “Implicitly”

Instead of a full “Fault Study,” the EE uses AIC (Ampere Interrupting Capacity) ratings.

The Logic: Rather than calculating the exact fault current, the engineer specifies that all new breakers (the 250A main and the 40A branches) must have an AIC rating higher than the utility’s available fault current (e.g., 65 kA AIC at 120/208 V).

The Proof: This is checked by the City Inspector in Phase 5 when they review the Short Circuit Current Rating (SCCR) on the equipment spec sheets, effectively replacing the need for a separate engineered fault current calculation document. The design basis (e.g., 42 kA available at MDP, 65 kAIC minimum) is stated on the one-line and in notes; the inspector verifies that installed equipment meets or exceeds that rating.

17.4 Fault Current Verification in Phase 6 (Utility Coordination)

When contacting the utility representative (e.g., for the Load Letter or service confirmation), you should request the Available Fault Current at the Transformer. If that number is higher than 10 kA (typical residential) or 22 kA (typical commercial), you must ensure the 250A EV feeder breaker is specified and installed with at least 65 kA AIC. For this reason, consider adding a “Fault Current Verification” task to Phase 6 (Utility Coordination): capture the utility-provided fault current value and confirm that the specified 65 kAIC minimum for new breakers remains adequate before equipment is ordered in Phase 7.

17.5 Phase 8 (Commissioning): Optional Fault Study & Arc-Flash Labels

A fault current study remains optional at commissioning but can be useful. Phase 8 is also when an electrician would apply arc-flash safety stickers to the equipment—if the building owner wants to be 100% OSHA-compliant for maintenance personnel. That labeling relies on an arc flash study performed with as-built data (see §17.2); if the owner opts for it, the study and sticker application are typically completed during or shortly after commissioning.
